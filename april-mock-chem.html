1.1

Atomic Structure

1.1.1 Fundamental Particles

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
Atoms consist of a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons. Know the relative charges and masses exactly — these are common fill-in-the-table questions.

Proton: relative mass = 1, charge = +1
Neutron: relative mass = 1, charge = 0
Electron: relative mass = 1/1836 (≈0), charge = −1
⚠️ Examiner Trap
Do NOT say electrons have "negligible mass" unless pushed — say relative mass ≈ 1/1836 or "approximately zero." AQA may penalise vague language. Also: electrons are in shells/orbitals around the nucleus, not "orbiting" like planets.
★ A* Insight
The discovery of subatomic particles evolved over time: Thomson's plum-pudding model → Rutherford's nuclear model (gold foil experiment) → Bohr's quantised shells → quantum mechanical model. AQA expects appreciation that atomic models develop as new evidence emerges.

1.1.2 Mass Number, Isotopes & TOF Mass Spectrometry

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons
Atomic number (Z) = number of protons
Isotopes = same element (same Z), different mass number (different neutron count). Same chemical properties (same electron config), different physical properties (different mass).
TOF Mass Spectrometer Steps (ELECTROSPRAY) 1. Ionisation: sample dissolved; H⁺ added → [M+H]⁺ ions formed
2. Acceleration: ions accelerated by electric field → same KE
3. Ion Drift: ions travel through field-free region (drift tube)
4. Detection: lighter ions arrive first (faster); detector records arrival time
5. Data Analysis: time → m/z ratio → mass
Relative Atomic Mass (from mass spectrum) Ar = Σ(isotope mass × relative abundance) / Σ(relative abundances)
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • In electrospray ionisation, ions formed are [M+H]⁺ not M⁺ — the m/z peak is one more than Mr.
  • Lighter ions travel faster and arrive at the detector first (inverse relationship).
  • Mass spectrometry gives m/z ratio, not just mass — most singly charged ions so z=1.
  • Relative isotopic mass ≠ mass number (real masses are not whole numbers).
★ A* Insight
All ions are given the same kinetic energy (½mv² = constant). Rearranging: v = √(2KE/m). Thus velocity is inversely proportional to √m. This is why TOF works — it's essentially a race where lighter ions win. This physics underpins the whole technique.

1.1.3 Electron Configuration & Ionisation Energies

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy: 1s 2s 2p 3s 3p 3d 4s 4p...
Within a subshell, electrons occupy orbitals singly before pairing (Hund's rule).

Key anomalies (Z ≤ 36): Cr = [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ (not 3d⁴ 4s²) and Cu = [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ (not 3d⁹ 4s²) — extra stability from half-full/full d subshells.

First ionisation energy: energy required to remove 1 mole of electrons from 1 mole of gaseous atoms to form 1 mole of +1 gaseous ions.
First IE equation (must write correctly) X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻ ΔH = 1st IE (always positive/endothermic)
Successive IEs — sharp jumps reveal shell structure Large jump between 2nd and 3rd IE → element is in Group 2
Large jump between 3rd and 4th IE → element is in Group 3
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • State symbols are essential — X(g) not X(s) or X(l) in IE equations.
  • IE always refers to gaseous atoms — never mention solution.
  • Mg has higher 1st IE than Al: Mg has full 3s² (harder to remove); Al has 3p¹ (shielded by 3s, easier to remove).
  • S has lower 1st IE than P: S has paired electron in 3p (repulsion makes removal easier).
  • Successive IEs always increase — never decrease.
★ A* Insight
The dip in 1st IE from Be→B and from N→O are AQA's favourite graph questions. Be→B: going from full 2s² to 2p¹ — 2p is higher energy and shielded by the 2s electrons. N→O: O has a paired 3p electron experiencing electron-electron repulsion, reducing the energy needed to remove it. Understand the trend, not just the exception.
1.2

Amount of Substance

1.2.1–1.2.2 Moles, Ar, Mr & Avogadro

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
Relative atomic mass (Ar): the weighted mean mass of an atom relative to 1/12 the mass of ¹²C.
Relative molecular mass (Mr): sum of all Ar values in a molecule (relative to ¹²C).
Relative formula mass: used for ionic compounds (not Mr).

1 mole = 6.02 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's constant, Nₐ) — given in exam.
Key Equations n = m / Mr   |   n = c × V   |   n = N / Nₐ
where n = moles, m = mass (g), Mr = relative molecular mass, c = concentration (mol dm⁻³), V = volume (dm³), N = number of particles
⚠️ Examiner Trap
Volume must be in dm³ for n = cV. 1 dm³ = 1000 cm³. Divide cm³ by 1000. This catches students every paper.
★ A* Insight
Ar values are not whole numbers because they are weighted means across isotopes. Carbon's Ar = 12.011, not exactly 12, because of the small natural abundance of ¹³C. This is why mass spectrometry is essential for precise measurement.

1.2.3 Ideal Gas Equation

▼
Ideal Gas Equation (R given in exam) pV = nRT
p = pressure (Pa), V = volume (m³), n = moles, R = 8.314 J mol⁻¹K⁻¹, T = temperature (K)
📖 Application
Finding Mr of volatile liquid: Weigh known mass of liquid → vaporise → measure V, T, p → calculate n from pV=nRT → Mr = m/n.
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • Pressure in Pa (not kPa or atm). 1 atm = 101,325 Pa; 100 kPa = 100,000 Pa.
  • Volume in m³ (not dm³ or cm³). 1 dm³ = 0.001 m³; 1 cm³ = 10⁻⁶ m³.
  • Temperature in Kelvin. T(K) = T(°C) + 273. Room temperature ≈ 293 K, not 293°C!

1.2.4–1.2.5 Empirical/Molecular Formula, Yield & Atom Economy RP1

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
Empirical formula: simplest whole number ratio of atoms.
Molecular formula: actual number of each atom.
Relationship: molecular formula = n × empirical formula (find n from Mr).
Key Equations % yield = (actual moles of product / theoretical moles) × 100

% atom economy = (Mr of desired product / sum of Mr of all reactants) × 100

Empirical formula method: divide % by Ar → divide by smallest → round to whole numbers
⚠️ Examiner Trap
Atom economy uses Mr of all reactants (including ones not in desired product), not just what's consumed. A 100% yield can still have poor atom economy if many by-products are formed.
★ A* Insight
Atom economy is a "green chemistry" metric. High atom economy reactions are better for sustainable chemistry because fewer resources are wasted and less waste disposal is required. AQA loves 6-mark evaluation questions on this.
⚗️ Required Practical 1: Titration
Make up a volumetric solution from a solid → pipette alkali into conical flask → add indicator → add acid from burette until colour change (end point) → record titre. Repeat for concordant results (within 0.10 cm³). Rinse burette with acid, pipette with alkali. Wash conical flask with distilled water only.
1.3

Bonding

1.3.1–1.3.3 Types of Chemical Bonding

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
Ionic: electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions in a lattice. Transfer of electrons. Metal + non-metal.

Covalent: shared pair of electrons between two atoms. Both atoms contribute one electron each. Shown by a line in structures.

Dative (coordinate) covalent: both electrons come from the same atom. Shown by an arrow (→). Same bond strength as ordinary covalent once formed. Examples: NH₄⁺ (from NH₃ + H⁺), Al₂Cl₆, H₃O⁺.

Metallic: delocalised electrons in a sea between positive metal ions arranged in a lattice.
⚠️ Examiner Trap
In NH₄⁺, once the dative bond forms, all N–H bonds become identical — there's no way to distinguish which one was dative. Students lose marks by implying one N–H bond is different. Also: BF₃ is electron-deficient (only 6 electrons around B), which is why it accepts a lone pair in dative bonding.
★ A* Insight
Metallic bonding strength increases with: (1) more delocalised electrons per atom (e.g. Mg²⁺ stronger than Na⁺), (2) smaller ionic radius (more charge density). This explains why Mg has a higher melting point than Na, and why Group 2 metals are generally harder and denser.

1.3.4 Crystal Structures & Physical Properties

▼
Diamond (macromolecular)
Each C bonded to 4 others (tetrahedral). Very high MP. No free e⁻ → no electrical conductivity. Hard. Used as abrasive.
Graphite (macromolecular)
Each C bonded to 3 others in layers. 1 delocalised e⁻ per C → conducts electricity. Layers held by van der Waals → slippery/lubricant. High MP (strong C-C bonds throughout).
Ice (molecular)
H₂O held by hydrogen bonds. Open lattice structure → lower density than liquid water. Relatively low MP.
Iodine (molecular)
I₂ molecules held by van der Waals forces. Low MP. Non-conductor. Slightly soluble in organic solvents.
NaCl (ionic)
6:6 coordination. High MP (strong electrostatic forces). Conducts when molten or dissolved (free ions). Doesn't conduct when solid.
Magnesium (metallic)
Mg²⁺ ions in sea of delocalised electrons. Good conductor. Malleable/ductile. Moderate-high MP.
⚠️ Common Misconception
Graphite conducts electricity because it has delocalised electrons — not because "layers can slide." Sliding refers to its lubricant properties (physical), not electrical conductivity. Keep these separate in your answers.

1.3.5 VSEPR & Molecular Shapes

▼
📖 Repulsion Order & Shapes
Lone pair–lone pair > lone pair–bond pair > bond pair–bond pair

2 bonding pairs: linear, 180°
3 bp, 0 lp: trigonal planar, 120° (e.g. BF₃)
4 bp, 0 lp: tetrahedral, 109.5° (e.g. CH₄)
3 bp, 1 lp: pyramidal, ~107° (e.g. NH₃)
2 bp, 2 lp: bent/V-shaped, ~104.5° (e.g. H₂O)
5 bp, 0 lp: trigonal bipyramidal, 90°/120°
6 bp, 0 lp: octahedral, 90°
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • Always state bond angle AND name of shape — one without the other loses marks.
  • CO₂ is linear (180°) — two double bonds, no lone pairs on C. Don't confuse with SO₂ which is bent (one lone pair on S).
  • PCl₅ is trigonal bipyramidal — there are TWO different bond angles (90° and 120°). Must state both.
  • In XeF₄ — 4 bp + 2 lp = square planar. The two lone pairs position themselves opposite each other to minimise repulsion.

1.3.6–1.3.7 Polarity & Intermolecular Forces

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
Electronegativity: power of an atom to attract the bonding pair in a covalent bond (Pauling scale). Increases across period, decreases down group. F > O > N > Cl > C.

Polar bond: unequal sharing → δ+ and δ- partial charges.
Polar molecule: polar bonds + asymmetric arrangement → net dipole.
Non-polar molecule: polar bonds + symmetric arrangement → dipoles cancel (e.g. CO₂, CCl₄).
📖 Intermolecular Forces (weakest → strongest)
van der Waals (London dispersion): all molecules. Caused by instantaneous dipoles inducing temporary dipoles. Strength increases with: more electrons, greater surface area, longer chain.

Permanent dipole-dipole: between polar molecules. Additional to vdW.

Hydrogen bonding: between H bonded to F, O, or N AND a lone pair on F, O, or N of another molecule. Strongest intermolecular force. Causes anomalously high BP of H₂O, HF, NH₃.
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • H-bonds form between H (bonded to F/O/N) and a lone pair on F/O/N — students often forget to mention the lone pair.
  • CO₂ is non-polar despite having polar C=O bonds because the molecule is linear and dipoles cancel. CCl₄ is similarly non-polar.
  • Ice is less dense than water because H-bonds hold molecules in an open lattice with more space between molecules.
  • van der Waals are present in ALL molecules, even non-polar ones. They are not exclusive to non-polar molecules.
★ A* Insight
The anomalously high boiling points of H₂O, HF, and NH₃ compared to their Group 6, 7, and 5 hydride series are direct evidence for hydrogen bonding. A* students can sketch a graph of BP vs period for Group 6 hydrides and explain the divergence at H₂O.
1.4

Energetics

1.4.1–1.4.2 Enthalpy Changes & Calorimetry RP2

▼
📖 Standard Conditions & Definitions
Standard conditions: 100 kPa pressure, specified temperature (usually 298 K).

ΔcH⊖ (standard enthalpy of combustion): ΔH when 1 mole of a substance is completely burned in oxygen under standard conditions. Always exothermic (negative).

ΔfH⊖ (standard enthalpy of formation): ΔH when 1 mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states. ΔfH⊖ of elements = 0 by definition.
Calorimetry q = mcΔT
q = heat change (J), m = mass of solution (g), c = specific heat capacity (J g⁻¹ K⁻¹, given), ΔT = temperature change (K or °C, same magnitude)

Then: ΔH = −q / n   (negative because exothermic reactions warm the surroundings)
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • Use mass of solution, not solute, in q=mcΔT. For dilute solutions, assume density ≈ 1 g/cm³.
  • The sign of ΔH: if temperature rises, reaction is exothermic → ΔH is negative.
  • Standard enthalpy of combustion must be for 1 mole of fuel, not products.
  • Calorimetry values are inaccurate because: heat is lost to surroundings, incomplete combustion, evaporation of fuel.
⚗️ Required Practical 2: Enthalpy by Calorimetry
Add measured volumes of solution to insulated polystyrene cup. Record initial temperatures. Mix solutions and record maximum (exothermic) or minimum (endothermic) temperature. Calculate q = mcΔT → ΔH per mole. Sources of error: heat loss to surroundings, incomplete reaction, thermometer accuracy.

1.4.3–1.4.4 Hess's Law & Bond Enthalpies

▼
📖 Hess's Law
The total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken — it depends only on the initial and final states (consequence of conservation of energy).

Via formation: ΔHreaction = ΣΔfH⊖(products) − ΣΔfH⊖(reactants)
Via combustion: ΔHreaction = ΣΔcH⊖(reactants) − ΣΔcH⊖(products) [note: reversed for products]
Bond Enthalpy Method ΔH = Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed)
Bond breaking: ENDOTHERMIC (+)
Bond forming: EXOTHERMIC (−)

Gives APPROXIMATE ΔH because mean bond enthalpies are averages across many different molecules/environments.
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • Bond enthalpy calculations are ALWAYS approximate because mean values are used. Hess's law gives exact values (for the specific compounds used).
  • In Hess's law via combustion: the formula is REVERSED — use combustion enthalpies of reactants MINUS products (not the other way around).
  • ΔfH⊖ of all elements in standard states = 0 (e.g. O₂(g), C(s) graphite, H₂(g)).
1.5

Kinetics

1.5.1–1.5.5 Collision Theory, M-B Distribution & Catalysts

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
Reactions occur when particles collide with sufficient energy (≥ activation energy, Ea) AND with the correct orientation.

Increasing rate: increase temperature → more particles have E ≥ Ea (and more frequent collisions); increase concentration/pressure → more frequent collisions; add catalyst → lowers Ea.

Catalyst: increases rate without being consumed. Provides alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy.
📖 Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution
Curve starts at origin (no molecules with zero energy), rises to a peak (most probable energy), then tails to the right (few high-energy molecules). Curve never touches x-axis at high energy end.

Effect of temperature increase: curve shifts right and flattens. Peak moves right. Area under curve = total number of molecules (constant). Area to the right of Ea increases → more molecules can react.
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • When explaining temperature effect on rate: must mention (1) more frequent collisions AND (2) greater proportion of molecules with E ≥ Ea. Many students only give one reason.
  • A catalyst lowers Ea — it does NOT increase the energy of the reactants.
  • On M-B diagrams: the curve must NOT touch the y-axis at zero (molecules have some minimum energy), and must NOT touch the x-axis (asymptotic).
  • The area under a M-B curve is constant (fixed number of molecules) — when temperature rises, the curve flattens but area stays the same.
  • Increasing concentration increases collision frequency, NOT the energy of collisions.
★ A* Insight
A small temperature increase (e.g. +10°C) causes a disproportionately large rate increase. This is because the M-B distribution has a tail: a small shift in temperature dramatically increases the proportion of molecules with E ≥ Ea. This non-linear relationship explains why biological enzymes operate in very narrow temperature ranges.
1.6

Equilibria & Kc

1.6.1–1.6.2 Le Chatelier's Principle & Kc

▼
📖 Dynamic Equilibrium
A reversible reaction at equilibrium has: forward and reverse rates equal; concentrations of reactants and products constant (not equal). The position can shift but Kc only changes with temperature.
📖 Le Chatelier's Principle
If a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it will shift to oppose the change.

Increase concentration of reactant: equilibrium shifts right (towards products).
Increase pressure: equilibrium shifts towards fewer moles of gas.
Increase temperature: equilibrium shifts in endothermic direction (absorbs heat). Also INCREASES Kc if forward reaction is endothermic, DECREASES Kc if exothermic.
Catalyst: equilibrium position unchanged; Kc unchanged; equilibrium reached faster.
Kc Expression — for aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ

Units: determined from the expression. e.g. if Kc = [C]²/[A][B], units = (mol dm⁻³)²/(mol dm⁻³)² = dimensionless
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • Kc is ONLY affected by temperature. Not by concentration, pressure, or catalyst.
  • A catalyst increases rate of BOTH forward and reverse reactions equally — position of equilibrium unchanged.
  • In industrial processes (e.g. Haber), the compromise conditions balance rate (kinetics) vs yield (thermodynamics). High pressure increases yield AND rate; high temperature increases rate but DECREASES yield for exothermic reactions.
  • Kc units must be calculated — do NOT assume dimensionless.
1.7

Oxidation, Reduction & Redox

1.7 Oxidation States & Half-Equations

▼
📖 Oxidation State Rules
1. Elements = 0
2. Simple ions = charge (e.g. Na⁺ = +1)
3. O = −2 (except peroxides: −1; OF₂: +2)
4. H = +1 (except metal hydrides: −1)
5. F = −1 always
6. Sum of ox states = overall charge of species
Half-Equations Method 1. Write species being oxidised/reduced
2. Balance atoms (add H₂O for O, H⁺ for H in acidic solution)
3. Balance charge by adding e⁻
4. Combine half-equations: cancel electrons

OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss (of e⁻), Reduction Is Gain (of e⁻)
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • Oxidation state of Cl in Cl₂ = 0, in HCl = −1, in NaClO = +1, in ClO₃⁻ = +5, in ClO₄⁻ = +7.
  • Mn in MnO₄⁻ = +7, in MnO₂ = +4, in Mn²⁺ = +2. Frequent source of marks.
  • In disproportionation, the same element is simultaneously oxidised and reduced (e.g. Cl₂ + NaOH).
  • When combining half-equations, multiply each so electrons cancel exactly — do NOT add electrons to both sides.
2.1

Periodicity

2.1.1 s, p, d, f Blocks & Classification

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
An element's block is determined by which subshell its highest energy electron occupies:
s-block: Groups 1 & 2 (last electron in s subshell)
p-block: Groups 3–7 & 0 (last electron in p subshell)
d-block (transition metals): filling 3d subshell (Sc–Zn, periods 4+)
f-block: lanthanides and actinides
⚠️ Examiner Trap
He is in Group 0 but is an s-block element (1s² configuration). H is also s-block. Zn and Cd: 3d¹⁰ full, technically d-block but often not classified as transition metals (no variable oxidation states in compounds).
2.2

Group 2 — Alkaline Earth Metals

2.2 Trends, Reactions & Applications

▼
📖 Trends (Mg → Ba)
Atomic radius: increases ↓ group (more shells)
1st IE: decreases ↓ group (greater shielding, larger radius, easier to remove electron)
Melting point: generally decreases ↓ group (less charge density → weaker metallic bonding), though not perfectly regular.
Reactions with Water Mg + H₂O → no reaction (cold water); Mg + H₂O(steam) → MgO + H₂
Ca + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂ (slow, some bubbling)
Sr/Ba + 2H₂O → Sr(OH)₂/Ba(OH)₂ + H₂ (vigorous)
📖 Hydroxide & Sulfate Solubility Trends
Hydroxides: solubility INCREASES down group. Mg(OH)₂ sparingly soluble → Ba(OH)₂ soluble.
Sulfates: solubility DECREASES down group. MgSO₄ soluble → BaSO₄ insoluble.

Uses: Mg(OH)₂ — antacid medicine. Ca(OH)₂ — agriculture (neutralise acidic soil). BaSO₄ — barium meal (X-ray contrast, safe due to insolubility). CaO/CaCO₃ — remove SO₂ from flue gases.
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • BaCl₂ test for sulfate ions must be acidified with dilute HCl to prevent false positives from Ba²⁺ reacting with CO₃²⁻ or SO₃²⁻.
  • Mg does NOT react with cold water — only steam.
  • Hydroxide solubility INCREASES down group; sulfate solubility DECREASES — students frequently confuse these two.
  • Mg is used to extract Ti from TiCl₄ (displacement): TiCl₄ + 2Mg → Ti + 2MgCl₂. Mg is the reducing agent.
3.1

Introduction to Organic Chemistry

3.1.1 Nomenclature & 3.1.2 Mechanisms

▼
📖 Representation Types
Empirical: simplest ratio (e.g. CH₂)
Molecular: actual numbers (e.g. C₄H₈)
General: CₙH₂ₙ for alkenes
Structural: CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃
Displayed: shows all bonds as lines
Skeletal: zig-zag, carbon at each vertex, H implied
📖 Curly Arrow Conventions
Curly arrow: shows movement of electron pair. Starts from lone pair or bond, points to where electrons go.
Fishhook arrow: shows movement of single electron (radical mechanisms).
Free radical mechanisms: do NOT use curly arrows — write equations only.
⚠️ Examiner Trap
Curly arrows must start from the correct place (lone pair or bond) and end at the correct place. Arrows pointing the wrong way lose marks. In radical mechanisms, explicitly show dots for unpaired electrons and write each step (initiation, propagation, termination) separately.

3.1.3 Isomerism — Structural & E/Z

▼
📖 Types of Isomerism
Structural isomers: same molecular formula, different structural formula. Types: chain (different carbon skeleton), position (same functional group, different position), functional group (different functional group).

Stereoisomers: same structural formula, different arrangement in space.
E/Z (geometric) isomerism: due to restricted rotation around C=C double bond. Requires two different groups on each carbon of the C=C. Use CIP priority rules (higher atomic number → higher priority) to assign E (opposite, Entgegen) or Z (same side, Zusammen).
⚠️ Examiner Trap
E/Z isomerism requires BOTH carbons of the double bond to have two DIFFERENT substituents. If one carbon has two identical groups (e.g. CH₂=), E/Z isomerism is not possible. Do not confuse E/Z with cis/trans — E/Z is the correct AQA terminology.
3.2

Alkanes

3.2.1–3.2.4 Cracking, Combustion & Free-Radical Substitution

▼
📖 Cracking
Thermal cracking: high temp (700–1200°C), high pressure, no catalyst. Produces alkenes (for polymer industry).
Catalytic cracking: high temp (~450°C), slight pressure, zeolite catalyst. Mainly produces motor fuels and aromatic hydrocarbons.
Economic reason: long-chain alkanes less in demand; cracking makes shorter, more useful chains.
Free-Radical Substitution of Methane + Cl₂ (UV light) INITIATION: Cl₂ → 2Cl• (UV breaks Cl-Cl bond homolytically)
PROPAGATION 1: Cl• + CH₄ → HCl + CH₃•
PROPAGATION 2: CH₃• + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl•
TERMINATION: Cl• + Cl• → Cl₂ (or CH₃• + Cl• → CH₃Cl, or CH₃• + CH₃• → C₂H₆)
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • The initiation step requires UV light/photons — thermal energy alone is insufficient.
  • In propagation, the radical is regenerated — this is how the chain continues.
  • Termination requires two radicals combining — write all three possible termination steps if asked.
  • Multiple products form (CH₂Cl₂, CHCl₃, CCl₄) because propagation can continue — this is a limitation of the reaction (poor selectivity).
  • Bond breaking in initiation is homolytic — each atom gets one electron. Write Cl• not Cl⁻.
3.3

Halogenoalkanes

3.3.1 Nucleophilic Substitution

▼
📖 Teaching Notes
C–X bond is polar (δ+C – δ–X) because X is more electronegative. This makes the C susceptible to attack by nucleophiles.

Nucleophile: species with a lone pair that donates electrons to form a bond. OH⁻, CN⁻, NH₃.

Reactions: with OH⁻ (aq) → alcohol; with CN⁻ → nitrile (chain lengthened by 1C); with NH₃ (excess) → amine.
📖 Effect of C–X Bond Enthalpy on Rate
C–F bond strongest → slowest hydrolysis
C–Cl bond
C–Br bond
C–I bond weakest → fastest hydrolysis
This is NOT about electronegativity — it's bond enthalpy (ease of bond breaking) that determines rate.
⚠️ Examiner Trap
Students often say "fluoroalkanes react fastest because F is most electronegative and the bond is most polar." This is WRONG. The C–F bond is so strong that despite being very polar, it hydrolyses slowest. Bond enthalpy determines rate, not polarity.

3.3.2–3.3.3 Elimination & Ozone Depletion

▼
📖 Elimination
Halogenoalkane + KOH(alc) + heat → alkene + KX + H₂O
KOH acts as a BASE (not nucleophile): accepts H⁺ from adjacent C while halide leaves. Requires concentrated KOH in ethanol (not aqueous — aqueous favours substitution).
CFC Ozone Depletion UV causes: CF₂Cl₂ → CF₂Cl• + Cl• (C–Cl bond breaks, not C–F — weaker bond)
Cl• + O₃ → ClO• + O₂
ClO• + O₃ → 2O₂ + Cl• (Cl• regenerated → catalytic cycle)
⚠️ Examiner Trap
Cl• is a catalyst — it is regenerated and not consumed overall. Each Cl• can destroy thousands of ozone molecules. C–Cl bond breaks (not C–F) because C–F is stronger. Must write Cl• with dot to show radical.
3.4

Alkenes

3.4.1–3.4.3 Structure, Addition Reactions & Polymers

▼
📖 Electrophilic Addition Mechanism
The C=C double bond has high electron density (π bond above and below the σ bond). This attracts electrophiles (electron-deficient species).

With Br₂: Br₂ is polarised by the π electrons → Brδ+–Brδ– → Brδ+ attacks C=C → bromonium intermediate → Br⁻ attacks → dibromoalkane. Bromine water decolorises (orange → colourless) = test for unsaturation.

With HBr: H⁺ attacks C=C → carbocation intermediate → Br⁻ attacks carbocation. For unsymmetrical alkenes: H⁺ adds to C with more H atoms (Markovnikov's rule) → forms more stable (more substituted) carbocation.
📖 Carbocation Stability
3° > 2° > 1° > methyl (primary)
More alkyl groups donate electrons → stabilise the positive charge → lower energy carbocation → forms preferentially (major product).
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • In electrophilic addition mechanisms, curly arrows must start from the π bond (or lone pair) and end at the electrophile. The arrow from C=C must originate from the bond, not from one carbon.
  • The major product in Markovnikov addition is determined by carbocation stability, not just "more hydrogens." Link explicitly to 3° > 2° > 1° stability.
  • H₂SO₄ addition: HSO₄⁻ is the nucleophile attacking the carbocation (not just "sulfuric acid adds").
★ A* Insight
Tertiary carbocations are more stable because alkyl groups are electron-donating (by hyperconjugation and inductive effect). This lowers the positive charge concentration on the central carbon. The more stabilised intermediate forms faster (lower activation energy), leading to the major product.

Fermentation
Glucose → ethanol + CO₂
C₆H₁₂O₆ → 2C₂H₅OH + 2CO₂
Conditions: yeast (enzyme), 25–35°C, anaerobic (no oxygen), pH ≈ 6. Ethanol separated by fractional distillation.
Catalytic Hydration
Ethene + steam → ethanol
CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → C₂H₅OH
Conditions: conc. H₃PO₄ catalyst, 300°C, 65 atm. Continuous process, higher purity.
📖 Oxidation of Alcohols
Oxidising agent: acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ (orange → green when reduced to Cr³⁺).

Primary alcohol → aldehyde (distil product off immediately) or
Primary alcohol → carboxylic acid (reflux with excess oxidising agent).
Secondary alcohol → ketone (reflux).
Tertiary alcohol → NO reaction (no H on carbon bearing OH).
📖 Distinguishing Aldehyde from Ketone
Tollens' reagent: silver mirror with aldehyde; no change with ketone.
Fehling's solution: brick-red precipitate with aldehyde; no change with ketone.
Both: aldehyde is oxidised to carboxylic acid; ketone cannot be oxidised under mild conditions.
⚠️ Examiner Traps
  • To obtain aldehyde: distil product immediately to remove it from the reaction mixture before it's further oxidised.
  • Tertiary alcohols do NOT react with acidified dichromate — no colour change is the observation.
  • Biofuel debate: ethanol from fermentation is NOT truly carbon neutral because of energy used in distillation, fertilisers, transport, and land use change. AQA expects a balanced evaluation.
⚗️ Required Practical 5: Distillation
Preparation of cyclohexene from cyclohexanol via acid-catalysed elimination. Setup: round-bottom flask, anti-bumping granules, thermometer at side arm, condenser, collection flask. Purify: separating funnel (wash with Na₂CO₃ to remove acid, then with CaCl₂ to remove water), dry with anhydrous CaCl₂, distil and collect fraction at correct BP.
